Overview

I am currently a PhD student in animal behaviour in the Department of Animal and Aqucultural Sciences, the Norwegian University of Life Sciences (Ås, Norway), finishing in November 2017.

My PhD is focused on the assessment of personality in dogs. I have a particular interest in the methods used to study personality, in dogs and other animals (human or non-human).

Why personality? Animal personality is the study of relatively consistent between-individual differences in behaviour. Like any scientific field, assumptions need to be made when studying personality, but it’s not always easy. Whilst everyone agrees that individuals differ from each other, it is harder to pinpoint exactly what personality is and how to scientifically investigate it. When studying personality, we are trying to understand how multiple biological and experiential factors combine to influence how and why individuals behave differently to each other - this is hard!

So far, I have been researching some of the conceptual foundations of measuring personality in dogs, and how we can improve the prediction of personality.

The main research questions of my PhD have been:

  • How robust are current methods to discover personality traits and dimensions in dogs?
  • How does personality interact with behavioural plasticity and day-to-day variation?
  • How can we statistically model longitudinal patterns of behavioural stability and change?
  • How can better predictions of future dog behaviour be made?

Other interests

Some of my other academic interests include:

  • Statistics, particularly the use of Bayesian statistics and multi-level modelling (e.g. using Stan). All statistical models are wrong (they are Golems), but I enjoy applying statistical models that have meaningful interpretations for research questions.

  • Philosophy of science, including the relationship between scientific realism and the complexity sciences. For instance, I am interested how unobservable phenomena can be represented or explained by the concept of emergent patterns.

  • Open and reproducible science. I endeavour to make my research as open as possible (open access, all data and code available to reproduce the results), although sometimes there are limitations to this (e.g. working with proprietary data). In future projects, I want to pre-register my hypotheses.

  • The complexity sciences
